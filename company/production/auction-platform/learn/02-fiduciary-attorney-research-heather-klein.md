## Why FHOA Needs a Fiduciary Attorney

The auction's financial architecture has three layers:

1. **Stripe** — processes credit card payments, handles PCI compliance, issues refunds
2. **Fiduciary attorney** — holds all funds in trust, executes disbursements, mediates high-value transactions
3. **Bank** — operating account where Stripe payouts land and expenses are paid

The fiduciary attorney is the critical layer. A bank cannot replace this role — bank escrow/trust services are institutional-scale (M&A, municipal bonds) and wrong-sized for this auction. The fiduciary needs to:

- Hold all auction funds in an IOLTA or dedicated trust account
- Confirm wire receipt for high-value bids and communicate status to the platform
- Execute immediate refunds when bidders are outbid
- Calculate and disburse outbid rewards (50/50 spread split) at settlement
- Reconcile settlement across up to 52 paintings with variable tier chains
- Provide bar association credentials for display on the auction site
- Advise on the credit card / wire threshold (~$5–10k)
- Potentially advise on securities-adjacent questions (Howey test on reward mechanic)

## Relevant Experience Assessment

### What real estate closing work gives her

Real estate attorneys routinely handle:
- **IOLTA trust accounts** — holding buyer funds until closing conditions are met
- **Escrow disbursement** — releasing funds to sellers, paying off liens, distributing proceeds per the closing statement
- **Wire transfers** — receiving and sending wires as part of closings
- **Title examination** — verifying ownership and encumbrances (less relevant here, but shows detail orientation)
- **Contract review** — ensuring terms are met before releasing funds
- **Regulatory compliance** — RESPA, state bar trust account rules

This is a solid foundation. The core mechanic — "hold money, verify conditions, release money" — is the same.

### Where the auction diverges from real estate closings

| Real Estate Closing | FHOA Auction |
|---|---|
| 2 parties (buyer + seller) | Potentially dozens of bidders + 1 artist |
| Single transaction | Up to 52 simultaneous paintings, each with cascading bid chains |
| Disbursement at one closing event | Immediate refunds on outbid + bulk settlement at auction close |
| Static dollar amounts | Dynamic pricing (tier upgrades change amounts mid-auction) |
| Established legal templates | Custom auction terms (no standard template exists) |
| No reward mechanics | 50/50 spread split creates outbound payments to outbid bidders |
| One-time event | One-time event (same) |
| Weeks of preparation | Auction runs for a defined period with real-time activity |

### Specific capability gaps to assess in conversation

1. **Multi-party disbursement at scale** — Can she handle 20–50 individual payouts in a compressed settlement window? Real estate closings are typically 2–4 payees.
2. **Ongoing escrow management** — During the auction (potentially weeks), she'd need to process refunds as bidders are outbid. This isn't "hold until closing" — it's active fund management.
3. **Wire confirmation turnaround** — High-value bids (Tier 2/3) require wire transfer. The platform needs confirmation within hours, not days. What's her responsiveness for wire receipt verification?
4. **Custom contract drafting** — The auction terms, bidder agreements, and escrow structure need to be drafted from scratch. No standard real estate template applies.
5. **Tax reporting** — Outbid rewards may trigger 1099 reporting if ≥$600. Is she set up for this?
6. **Securities awareness** — The reward mechanic could be scrutinized under the Howey test. She doesn't need to be a securities lawyer, but she needs to flag the question and know when to refer out.
7. **Technology comfort** — The fiduciary interacts with the platform (confirming wire receipt via admin interface, possibly receiving webhook notifications). How comfortable is she with web-based tools?
